# Scientific Writing Rules

## 1. General Principles

- Use simple and direct sentences.
- Prefer short sentences over long and complex ones.
- Avoid unnecessary punctuation.
- Avoid decorative or redundant expressions.
- Each sentence must convey one clear idea.

- The text must be easy to read and unambiguous.
- Avoid vague terms such as "various", "some", "many" unless necessary.
- Prefer concrete and precise wording.

---

## 2. Global Coherence

- The document must be consistent as a whole.
- Each section must be aligned in tone, style, and level of detail.

- When adding or modifying a section:
  - Re-read the entire document.
  - Ensure consistency with previous sections.
  - Remove contradictions.
  - Remove duplicated concepts.

- Avoid sections that look pasted without integration.
- Ensure smooth transitions between paragraphs and sections.

---

## 3. Redundancy and Continuity

- Avoid repeating the same concept in multiple parts of the document.
- If repetition is necessary, rephrase and reduce it.

- Each paragraph must:
  - Start with a clear purpose.
  - Connect with the previous paragraph.
  - Lead naturally to the next one.

- Avoid isolated paragraphs with no clear role.

---

## 4. Structure

Every document must have a clear structure.

### For general documents (e.g., README, project docs):
- Introduction
- Context or problem
- Description of the solution
- Details (optional)
- Conclusion or next steps

### For scientific papers:
- Introduction
- Related Work
- Method / Approach
- Experimental Evaluation
- Discussion
- Conclusion

---

## 5. Introduction (Scientific Papers)

- Clearly state:
  - The problem
  - Why it matters
  - The gap in the literature

- Avoid generic openings.
- Be specific and concrete.

- Explicitly list the contributions:
  - Use a short bullet list
  - Each contribution must be clear and verifiable

---

## 6. Related Work

- Do not just list papers.
- Compare existing approaches.

- Highlight:
  - Differences from your work
  - Limitations of prior work

- Use a consistent structure for comparisons.

---

## 7. Method / Approach

- Explain the idea before the details.
- Provide intuition before formal definitions.

- Each concept must be introduced before being used.
- Avoid jumps in logic.

- Use examples when useful.

---

## 8. Experimental Evaluation

- Clearly describe:
  - Setup
  - Data
  - Metrics

- Ensure reproducibility.

- Do not hide negative results if they are relevant.

---

## 9. Writing Style

- Use active voice when possible.
- Avoid overly complex grammar.

- Avoid:
  - Long nested clauses
  - Excessive use of commas
  - Unnecessary adjectives

- Keep terminology consistent throughout the document.

---

## 10. Reviewer-Oriented Writing

- Assume the reader is a reviewer.

- The reviewer:
  - Has limited time
  - Will form an opinion quickly

- Therefore:
  - Make contributions explicit
  - Avoid ambiguity
  - Do not leave key points implicit

- The paper must be self-contained:
  - Do not assume missing information will be inferred

---

## 11. Integrity and Ethics

- Do not copy text without proper citation.
- Do not paraphrase without attribution.

- Avoid self-plagiarism:
  - Do not reuse your previous text as new content without citation

- Do not fabricate or manipulate data.

- All claims must be supported by:
  - Evidence
  - References
  - Experiments

---

## 12. Citations

- Cite relevant work fairly.
- Do not overuse self-citations.

- Citations must:
  - Support claims
  - Provide context
  - Acknowledge prior work

---

## 13. Final Review Process

Before finalizing the document:

- Read the document from start to end.
- Check:
  - Coherence
  - Flow
  - Redundancy
  - Clarity

- Remove:
  - Repeated ideas
  - Unnecessary sentences
  - Inconsistent terminology

- Ensure that:
  - Each section has a clear role
  - The document reads as a single coherent text
